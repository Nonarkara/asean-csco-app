Part 1: The Foundation - A New Philosophy for Smart Cities
Introduction: Escaping the Smart City Paradox
As an urban leader in ASEAN today, you stand at a crossroads. The promise of technology is immense, yet the path to a truly "smart city" is littered with expensive failures and forgotten pilot projects. This is the Smart City Paradox: most frameworks are built on theory, not practice. This creates a fundamental disconnect between the technology we deploy and the citizens it is meant to serve. This handbook is your guide to escaping that paradox.
The traditional, top-down approach to building a smart city has consistently failed, typically in three ways:
Top-Down Solutions: Cities chase technological trends, buying the latest gadgets instead of solving the real, everyday frustrations of their people. The focus is on technology, not on citizen value.
High Cost, Low Trust: Municipalities invest in expensive, complex systems that the public neither understands nor trusts. Without buy-in, these projects fail to deliver tangible benefits and become monuments to wasted resources.
Slow Implementation: Bureaucratic processes and lengthy procurement cycles kill momentum. The old way of working—where fixing a simple pothole could take up to 15 days of paperwork—is too slow for the digital age. In a citizen-first city, that same pothole is fixed in 48 hours.
This handbook is the antidote. It offers a proven, replicable system built on a citizen-first philosophy. We will not explore abstract theories. Instead, we will unpack the living blueprint of Nakhon Si Thammarat, a historic Thai city that transformed its governance, culture, and technology by putting its people first.
This is the story of their journey, and the playbook for yours.

The Blueprint: The Story of Nakhon Si Thammarat
The best lessons are not learned from theory, but from reality. The journey of Nakhon Si Thammarat (NST) is a compelling narrative of how a historic, second-tier city facing significant challenges became a global benchmark for citizen-centric innovation. Their success is not about having a massive budget; it is about a fundamentally different approach to governance.
A City Overlooked
Nakhon Si Thammarat is a city of approximately 105,000 people, with a cultural lineage tracing back 1,200 years. Rich in heritage, it was also a city facing modern adversity. Repeated flooding, stagnant economic growth, and a lack of digital investment left it vulnerable. This perception was compounded by deep structural issues like centralization, infrastructure gaps, and poor data governance. The city was often overlooked by investors and policymakers—perceived as too provincial, too complex, and too hard to change.
The Rise of a Leader
The city’s trajectory changed with the election of Mayor Dr. Kanop Ketchart. Born into a legacy of public service as the son of one of Thailand’s longest-serving mayors, he combines a PhD in engineering from the United States with experience in both public works and the private sector. But his path to leadership was not direct.
After a controversial electoral defeat in 2011, where over 21,000 ballot stubs were reported missing, he did not retreat. Instead, he spent the next several years walking from neighborhood to neighborhood, listening. As one resident recalled, “He didn’t come to promise things... He just sat down, asked questions, and remembered our names.” This period of grounding built a deep, authentic trust with the community. When he ran again in 2021, the result was a decisive mandate: he led his team to victory, sweeping all 24 seats in the city council.
A Strategic Partnership
That victory found its catalyst in a pivotal meeting in 2021. Dr. Kanop traveled to Bangkok to receive certification for Nakhon Si Thammarat as a Smart City Promotional Zone from Thailand’s Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa). He arrived not just with ambition, but with a sincere curiosity to learn. He had a vision, but he needed a framework. This meeting marked a pivot, initiating a three-year journey of co-design, co-funding, and capacity building that transformed NST's ambition into a structured path to success.
NST’s success was not an accident. It was built on a clear, layered model of transformation that any city can learn from.

The NST Model: Four Layers of Transformation
Nakhon Si Thammarat’s success is not magic. It is a replicable model built upon four distinct but interconnected layers. This framework moves from the visible solutions citizens see every day to the foundational governance that makes it all possible. Understanding this model is the first step to adapting it for your own city.
Source: Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa) and Ministry of Digital Economy and Society (2025). Citizen-First City Blueprint: An ASEAN Handbook for Leaders, Innovators, and Citizens. Slides generated via NotebookLM.
Layer 1: Visible Innovation This is the outer layer—the tangible, citizen-facing solutions that solve real, everyday problems. In NST, technology is a tool, not the goal. The solutions are designed to address what people actually face. The most potent example? Reducing the time it takes to fix a reported pothole from 15 days to just 48 hours.
The @NakhonCity platform: This platform turned citizens into active participants. With over 70% of the population on it, the city has resolved 38,000 complaints in the last three years, saving an estimated $500,000 in operational costs.
Hospital on Wheels: Inspired by models in South Korea, a smart bus equipped with doctors, nurses, and pharmacists brings healthcare directly to the doorsteps of 60,000 residents, many of whom are elderly or have disabilities and cannot easily travel to the city hospital.
Flood Management: Instead of a complex system, the city installed real-time, publicly accessible water-level cameras. This simple solution gives citizens a 10-hour 'golden period' to prepare for floods, empowering them to see the situation with their own eyes.

Layer 2: The People-Centric Philosophy This is the "why" behind the technology. It is a mindset that prioritizes human needs and behavioral change over technical specifications.
The 'Clever Cats' Analogy: Mayor Kanop uses a simple story to explain his leadership philosophy. "You drag a cat forward, it will go backward. It resists change if you push or force. But if you are a stupid cat, if a mayor is a stupid cat, all the clever cats want to follow." The principle is to lead by showing, not by mandating.
An 'Eatable' Smart City: The goal is to make complex technology digestible. Solutions are translated into simple, tangible benefits that every citizen can understand, use, and feel the impact of in their daily lives.

Layer 3: The Collaborative Ecosystem Success was not achieved in isolation. It required a dynamic public-private partnership that combined local knowledge, technical expertise, and strategic guidance. The key players were:
City Leadership (Mayor Kanop): Provided essential local knowledge, political will, and a deep understanding of citizen pain points.
Digital Startups: Delivered agile, cost-effective, and context-aware solutions. The @NakhonCity platform was built by a small local startup that understood local needs, getting the project off the ground faster.
National Agency (depa): Offered strategic guidance, ecosystem support, and connected the city with key players, helping them avoid costly solutions from big companies.

Layer 4: Empowered Local Governance This is the core—the foundational political structure that makes everything else possible. A 1997 constitutional reform in Thailand fundamentally changed the power of local governments.
Before 1997 (Indirect Election): Mayors were chosen by a municipal assembly. Power was diffuse, leading to short tenures and limited accountability to voters.
After 1997 (Direct Election & 'Strong Mayor' System): Directly elected mayors gained executive power, secure tenures, and control over budgets and personnel. This created a new system designed for stability and direct accountability to citizens.
This four-layer model provides the foundation for a repeatable strategy. The four layers represent the city's foundational structure and philosophy. In the next part, we will deconstruct the four pillars—the actionable, step-by-step process any leader can follow to build on that foundation and achieve similar results.

Part 2: The Citizen-First Playbook - The Four Pillars in Action
Pillar 1: People - Start with Pain, Not Technology
The first and most critical pillar of a citizen-first city is to ground every initiative in the real, lived frustrations of your people. The journey does not begin with a request for proposals or a technology catalog. It begins with a simple question: What is broken?

Source: Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa) and Ministry of Digital Economy and Society (2025). Citizen-First City Blueprint: An ASEAN Handbook for Leaders, Innovators, and Citizens. Slides generated via NotebookLM.
Core Principle: Identify Citizen Frustration
As a city leader, your first and most critical task is to identify what frustrates your citizens today. Do not chase trends or buy technology for its own sake. Use direct feedback, data from your call centers, and on-the-ground observation to find the daily friction points in your city. This approach ensures you are solving problems that matter, not just problems that are technologically interesting.
Case Study Application: The Birth of the @NakhonCity platform
The @NakhonCity platform was not born in a boardroom. It began as a direct response to citizen frustrations with an inefficient, paper-based reporting system. Reporting a pothole, a broken streetlight, or an overflowing garbage bin involved a slow, bureaucratic process that left residents feeling unheard. By starting with this specific pain, the city ensured its first major digital initiative would deliver immediate, visible value to its people.
Once you have identified a genuine pain point, the next step is to define a clear and human-centered purpose for solving it.
Pillar 2: Purpose - Define the Human Benefit
A clear purpose is your compass. It ensures that every project delivers a tangible benefit to people, rather than simply meeting a technical specification on a procurement document. It transforms the conversation from what you are buying to who you are helping.
Core Principle: From Pain Point to Purpose
The goal is to translate a citizen's frustration into a clear, human-centered objective. This requires shifting your team’s mindset away from asking, "What technology should we buy?" Instead, you must ask, "What benefits can our people get?" A strong purpose statement is simple, measurable, and focused on a human outcome.
Case Study Application: The Hospital on Wheels
The purpose of the Hospital on Wheels project was never to deploy an IoT-enabled bus. The purpose was to solve the pain point for 60,000 elderly or disabled residents who could not easily travel to the city hospital. Inspired by mobile service models observed in South Korea, the human benefit was clear and direct: bring doctors, nurses, and pharmacists to their doorsteps. By defining the project in these terms, the city focused on outcomes—better health access, reduced travel burdens—rather than outputs.
A powerful purpose must be paired with a realistic plan for execution. This is the focus of the next pillar: practicality.
Pillar 3: Practicality - Use What You Have, Build What You Can
Implementation is where many smart city visions die. Practicality demystifies this stage. It means focusing on what is achievable now with the resources you have, rather than waiting for a perfect, large-scale solution that may never arrive.
Core Principle: Focus on the Achievable
From one practitioner to another, let me be clear: practicality is about thinking what it takes to get to what you want. This involves using only the data you truly need and building systems you can build today. The rise of no-code development tools and the abundance of local talent mean you don't need a massive budget or a global consulting firm to get started. Leverage what you have.
Case Study Application: A Local Ecosystem
To build the initial @NakhonCity platform, Nakhon Si Thammarat did not hire a big, expensive company. Instead, it partnered with a small, local startup that understood the city's context. This approach was more cost-effective, faster, and resulted in a solution better tailored to local needs. This practical step was supported by the national agency, depa, whose Digital Catalog provides a pre-vetted list of Thai tech vendors. This tool simplifies procurement, reduces risk, and makes it easier for cities to find practical, trusted partners.
With a practical plan in place, the final step before a full-scale launch is to test your idea in the real world to prove its value.
Pillar 4: Proof - Test Small, Learn Fast, Build Trust
Pilot projects are your most powerful tool for innovation. They should not be seen as final products, but as strategic experiments designed for learning. A successful pilot earns trust, secures buy-in from citizens and internal stakeholders, and provides the evidence you need to scale with confidence.

Source: Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa) and Ministry of Digital Economy and Society (2025). Citizen-First City Blueprint: An ASEAN Handbook for Leaders, Innovators, and Citizens. Slides generated via NotebookLM
Core Principle: From Purpose to Pilot
Adopt the mantra: "Launch pilots in weeks, not years. Test small, fail fast, and iterate." In the early stages, momentum matters more than perfection. A small, successful pilot is the most powerful tool to win over skeptics and demonstrate tangible value. It is the proof that turns an idea into a credible solution.
Case Study Application: The Evolution of the @NakhonCity platform
The first version of the @NakhonCity platform was a simple proof-of-concept. It was not perfect, but it was real. Crucially, the city didn't stop there. Based on real user feedback gathered during the pilot phases, the app was rebuilt twice in three years. Each iteration made the platform more robust and more responsive to citizen needs. This iterative process provided the hard evidence needed to justify building a more comprehensive, city-wide solution.
Once a pilot has proven its value, the challenge shifts from testing an idea to scaling a solution across the city.

Part 3: From Blueprint to Reality - Scaling and Adaptation
From Pilot to Scale: Embedding the Solution
Scaling a successful pilot is about more than just wider deployment. True scaling means embedding the solution into the core operations and culture of your city. It is the final step in moving from a promising project to a new way of governing.
Core Principle: From Solution to Scale
This stage requires training staff, adjusting internal workflows, and ensuring the solution is financially and operationally sustainable for the long term. The most essential fuel for scaling is trust—the trust you earned from citizens, staff, and political leaders through the pilot's success. It is this trust that allows you to make the solution a permanent part of how the city runs.
Analysis: Making the @NakhonCity platform Essential
Nakhon Si Thammarat successfully scaled its platform by making it central to municipal operations. It became the primary channel for issue reporting and service delivery. The results were clear: the city’s call center workload dropped by 37%, and quantifiable operational savings were achieved. The solution was no longer just an app; it had become part of the city's nervous system.
Successful scaling depends on a strong support network. The final challenge is to build a collaborative ecosystem and adapt this model to your city's unique context.

Appendix 1 : Supporting Frameworks and In-Depth Analysis for Smart City Transformation
1.0 The Regional and National Smart City Ecosystem
The success of any municipal smart city initiative is rarely achieved in isolation. It depends heavily on a supportive architecture of regional agreements and national policies that create an enabling environment for innovation. Understanding this broader ecosystem is critical, as it provides the strategic direction, technical frameworks, and financial incentives that empower local governments to transform ambitious visions into tangible realities. The following analysis details the key regional and national frameworks that have shaped and supported the urban transformation efforts in Thailand, including those of Nakhon Si Thammarat.
1.1 The ASEAN Strategic Vision: ASCAP 2026-2035
The ASEAN Smart City Action Plan (ASCAP) 2026-2035 represents the region's collective commitment to leveraging technology and innovation to address urban challenges. Aligned with the broader ASEAN Community Vision 2045, the plan envisions empowering cities and villages to enhance quality of life, stimulate economic growth, and prioritize environmental sustainability. Its strategic goal is to foster a community capable of managing urban and rural challenges through modern technology and innovative infrastructure.
The ASCAP is structured around four primary envisioned outcomes:
Enhanced urban and rural quality of life through improved public services and connectivity.
Advanced sustainability and resilience to address climate change and environmental pressures.
Strengthened regional collaboration and inclusivity, ensuring that the benefits of smart development are shared across the community.
Promoted economic growth and innovation, creating new opportunities and fostering a competitive digital economy.
1.2 Core Elements of the ASEAN Action Plan
The ASCAP outlines a comprehensive strategy through ten core elements, each with specific actions and clear objectives. These elements provide a guiding framework for member states and cities to align their local development priorities with regional aspirations.
1.3 Thailand's National Smart City Framework: The Role of depa and the SCO
At the national level, Thailand has established a robust institutional framework to translate regional visions into local action. The Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa) and its dedicated Smart City Thailand Office (SCO) are the primary engines driving this agenda, functioning as crucial enablers for municipalities across the country.
Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa)
Established in 2017, depa is the pivotal institution responsible for promoting Thailand's digital economy and guiding its national smart city development. For cities like Nakhon Si Thammarat, depa has evolved from a regulatory body into a hands-on strategic partner, providing the frameworks, funding, and expertise needed to accelerate local transformation. Its key support mechanisms include:
The Digital Catalog: A pre-vetted list of Thai technology vendors and digital solutions that simplifies procurement for municipalities. This tool significantly reduces procurement cycles and de-risks digital investments by offering ready-to-contract options.
Technical Mentorship and Capacity Building: The agency offers direct mentorship to city officials on critical topics such as user-centered design, data governance, and ethical AI procurement, bridging knowledge gaps within local administrations.
Smart City Thailand Office (SCO)
Operating under depa, the SCO is the specialized unit that manages the national smart city agenda. It provides the strategic direction, technical tools, and financial incentives necessary for cities to navigate their complex transformation journeys. Key mechanisms include:
Smart City Promotional Zone Certification: This official designation unlocks access to co-funding opportunities, strategic guidance, and technical support, signaling a city's readiness for digital transformation.
Pilot Funding and Co-Financing Programs: The SCO offers grants for early-stage smart city projects, reducing the initial financial burden on municipal budgets and allowing cities to test new technologies and services before committing to large-scale deployments.
This robust national architecture provided the necessary tailwinds, but the transformation in Nakhon Si Thammarat would ultimately be determined by the specific chemistry of its local leadership and its unique historical context.

2.0 Deep Dive: The Nakhon Si Thammarat Context
To truly understand a city's smart transformation, one must look beyond the technical blueprints and policy documents. The story behind the blueprint—the unique history, the character of its leadership, and the specific local challenges that catalyzed change—is essential for interpreting its journey. The case of Nakhon Si Thammarat (NST) is not just about technology; it is a narrative of resilience, trust-building, and strategic partnership that laid the groundwork for innovation.
2.1 A City Profile: From Historical Vulnerability to Renewed Vision
Nakhon Si Thammarat is a significant second-tier urban center in Southern Thailand, serving as a regional hub for culture, religion, and commerce.
Area: 22.56 square kilometers
Population: Approximately 105,000 residents
Population Density: Approximately 4,650 people per square kilometer
Economy: A blend of traditional sectors and emerging digital industries
Despite its rich heritage, the city has historically faced significant adversity. For years, it was perceived as a provincial city vulnerable to both climate and irrelevance. Repeated, severe flooding regularly disrupted daily life, while stagnant economic growth and a lack of long-term digital investment left it overlooked by investors and policymakers. These deep-seated challenges created a powerful impetus for fundamental change.
2.2 The Catalyst for Change: Leadership and Grounded Trust
The city's trajectory shifted decisively with the election of Mayor Dr. Kanop Ketchart. His path to leadership, however, was not direct and ultimately became a defining element of his governance style. After an unsuccessful first mayoral run in 2011, which was marred by electoral irregularities, Dr. Kanop chose not to retreat. Instead, he spent the next several years walking through the city's neighborhoods, not to campaign, but to listen. This period of deep community engagement built a foundation of trust that political promises alone could not. As one street vendor recalled, “He didn’t come to promise things... He just sat down, asked questions, and remembered our names.”
This groundwork proved decisive. In 2021, he ran again and won a sweeping victory, securing all 24 seats in the city council. He was re-elected in another landslide in 2025. His leadership—a unique combination of academic rigor, bureaucratic experience, and entrepreneurial spirit—was legitimized by the authentic public trust he had cultivated over years of patient, on-the-ground engagement.
2.3 The Power of Partnership: The NST-depa Co-Design Journey
This new leadership found a powerful ally in the Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa). A formal meeting in 2021, following Mayor Kanop’s election, marked a pivotal moment. The Mayor, long interested in smart city concepts, came to the meeting not just for certification but with a sincere curiosity to learn. He presented an early, rudimentary app mockup, a gesture that signaled his genuine intent. A depa officer later reflected on this first encounter: “It was cartoonish, almost naïve. But it was sincere. He wasn’t selling us something. He was asking how to make it real.”
This meeting catalyzed a three-year journey of co-design, co-funding, and capacity building. The relationship transformed depa's role from a high-level certifying body into an active, hands-on enabler. With depa's support, NST embedded user-centered design into its policy execution, ensuring that digital solutions were developed with citizens, not just for them. This partnership became the engine driving the city's ability to translate its renewed vision into practical initiatives, leveraging the very tools depa was designed to provide—from pilot funding that de-risked early experiments to the Digital Catalog that streamlined procurement.
With the foundational elements of trusted leadership and strategic partnership firmly in place, the city could now construct the technical and analytical systems required to turn its citizen-centric vision into a functional reality.

3.0 Technical Blueprints and Methodological Analyses
This section provides a technical and academic examination of the core components of Nakhon Si Thammarat's transformation. It moves beyond the narrative to analyze the city's digital backbone—the platform that connects citizens to services—and its underlying urban structure, offering a deeper understanding of the systems that support its smart city initiatives.
3.1 The Digital Backbone: An In-Depth Look at the '@NakhonCity' Platform
The rapid adoption and impact of the @NakhonCity platform were built on the foundation of public trust meticulously cultivated by Mayor Kanop in the years preceding its launch. Initiated in 2021, the @NakhonCity platform (powered by the MyCity system) began as a straightforward civic feedback application, allowing residents to report issues like streetlight outages or garbage collection delays. However, it quickly evolved from this simple tool into the comprehensive digital backbone of the city's governance. By mid-2023, it integrated modules for public safety alerts, real-time air quality monitoring, and appointment booking for public services.
This evolution from a feedback loop to a "feedback culture" has produced measurable results, demonstrating the platform's impact on municipal efficiency and citizen satisfaction.
The municipal call center workload dropped by 37% compared to pre-app levels.
The city achieved annual savings of over ฿2.3 million in operational inefficiencies.
User satisfaction, measured via in-app surveys, rose from 68% in 2022 to 92% in 2024.
In 2024, the city began layering a strategic layer of predictive analytics onto the platform's data. For example, analysis of repeated water leakage reports led to the pre-emptive replacement of aging pipes, while traffic jam heatmaps informed new signal-timing algorithms. This shift from reactive problem-solving to proactive, data-driven prevention marks a significant maturation of the city's digital capabilities.
3.2 Urban Morphology and Development: A Spatial Analysis of NST
An academic study analyzing Nakhon Si Thammarat's urban morphology provides critical insights into its physical structure and historical development. The research employed Space Syntax and Geographic Information Systems (GIS) to analyze the city's linear urban expansion along a north-south axis over three distinct phases: pre-1965, 1965, and 1999.
The study's primary objective was to determine how this expansion affected the spatial centrality and utilization of the historic Tha Wang old town area. The analysis concluded that, contrary to what might be expected, the linear expansion of the city's road network alone did not significantly impact the utilization of the old town. This finding suggests that other socio-economic factors, such as rental prices or area management policies, are more influential in shaping the vitality of the historic core.
The study measured two key coefficients to assess the spatial network's characteristics across the three phases: Synergy (the correlation between local and global connectivity) and Intelligibility (how easily the whole city layout can be understood from its parts).
These digital backbones and structural analyses, while critical, are merely enablers; their true value is measured by the tangible, on-the-ground projects they empower to improve citizens' daily lives.

1.0 The Foundation of Trust: Testimonies on Leadership
The strategic importance of leadership in Nakhon Si Thammarat's transformation cannot be overstated. The election of Mayor Dr. Kanop Ketchart marked a significant "change in trajectory" for a city that had faced stagnation. His approach was pivotal in overcoming historical inertia and building citizen trust—the necessary capital to launch any successful civic initiative. However, his personal leadership was amplified by a systemic change in governance that made such a transformation possible.
This structural shift was the foundational layer of NST's success. The 1997 Thai Constitution initiated major decentralization reforms, creating a "Strong Mayor" system where mayors are directly elected by voters. This fundamentally changed the power of local governments, giving directly elected leaders executive authority, control over budgets, and direct accountability to citizens. This empowered local governance model created the conditions for a leader like Dr. Kanop to drive sustained change, turning his personal vision into an actionable, city-wide mandate.
From Defeat to Grounding
Dr. Kanop's path to the mayorship was not immediate. After his first run in 2011 ended in defeat under controversial circumstances—with over 21,000 ballot stubs reported missing, casting serious doubt on the election's integrity—he did not withdraw. Instead, he channeled his determination into a years-long period of listening. He walked from neighborhood to neighborhood, engaging with residents without a campaign agenda. This quiet, consistent presence built a deep reservoir of goodwill. A local street vendor's memory captures the essence of his approach:
"He didn’t come to promise things. He just sat down, asked questions, and remembered our names."
This testimony highlights a shift from transactional politics to relational leadership. This long-term engagement forged a bond of trust that proved far more powerful than any campaign promise. This authenticity was the critical factor that led to his decisive electoral victories in 2021 and 2025, providing him with the strong public mandate needed to fully activate the power of his office and drive meaningful change.
This foundation of trust enabled the city to move away from traditional, top-down governance and embrace a new, collaborative approach to solving its most pressing problems.
2.0 The Shift to Co-Creation: Testimonies from the Community
Nakhon Si Thammarat's strategic shift from conventional top-down planning to a citizen-centric, co-design model was a cornerstone of its transformation. Rather than prescribing solutions, the city's leadership and its partners prioritized asking what residents needed and empowering them as active participants. This section presents testimonies from community members, illustrating how the smart city initiatives were not about technology for its own sake, but about grounding every solution in the real-world problems and aspirations of the people.
Solving Real Pain Points
The city's commitment to starting with citizen needs is clearly evidenced by the feedback from local educators. Before launching ambitious programs, the municipality engaged with stakeholders to understand their primary frustrations. This methodology directly reflects a core principle of the city's operational playbook, "Commandment #2: Solve Pain Points First." A local secondary school teacher’s experience reveals the power of this simple, yet often overlooked, first step:
"I don’t think anyone ever asked us what we needed before. When we got the internet in our school through the pilot project, the first thing the kids asked was: Can we learn how to make games?"
This quote is a powerful reminder that true innovation begins with empathy. By listening first, the city ensured its digital initiatives were not just about deploying infrastructure but about opening doors to opportunities that were genuinely meaningful to the community. This principle of addressing citizen pain points became a non-negotiable tenet for all subsequent projects.
Fostering Local Opportunity
The smart city strategy also had a tangible economic impact, creating an ecosystem that could nurture and retain local talent. The testimony from a local tech entrepreneur powerfully illustrates the journey from a fragmented, resource-scarce environment to one that actively supports innovation.
"I started as a freelance designer and had to use a coffee shop Wi-Fi for my first client pitch. Now we’re part of the Smart City Incubator program. I want to hire, stay here, and grow here."
This statement is a key indicator of success. It demonstrates that the smart city initiatives have moved beyond public service delivery to create a tangible local innovation ecosystem. By providing infrastructure, mentorship, and a supportive network, the city is enabling entrepreneurs to build their futures in Nakhon Si Thammarat, reversing brain drain and fostering sustainable, homegrown economic development.
The perspectives of the community were essential, but their vision could only be realized through the dedicated work of the officials and partners responsible for its execution.

3.0 The Internal Perspective: Testimonies on Process and Partnership
The success of Nakhon Si Thammarat's transformation was not just the result of external-facing projects; it was equally dependent on a profound shift in internal culture and the cultivation of strategic partnerships. This dynamic represents the "Collaborative Ecosystem" layer of the city's model, a dynamic public-private partnership combining local knowledge, technical expertise, and strategic guidance. The collaboration between the municipality and its key national partner, the Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa), was instrumental. The testimonies in this section reveal the practical and philosophical underpinnings of this partnership.
A Partnership Built on Sincere Vision
The pivotal 2021 meeting between the newly elected Mayor Kanop and depa officials set the tone for their collaboration. The mayor arrived not with a polished proposal, but with a sincere, if rudimentary, prototype that conveyed his genuine vision. A depa officer recalled the encounter:
"When we first met, Dr. Kanop brought an app mockup to the table. It was cartoonish, almost naïve. But it was sincere. He wasn’t selling us something. He was asking how to make it real."
This testimony highlights the power of authenticity in forging productive partnerships. The mayor's humility created an environment of trust, signaling that the city was a genuine partner committed to co-learning. This relationship became the cornerstone of the NST-depa ecosystem, which de-risked innovation for the municipality. depa provided a "Digital Catalog" of pre-vetted vendors to simplify and accelerate procurement, offered co-funding for pilot projects, and supplied crucial technical mentorship, allowing the city to experiment and iterate at a pace traditional bureaucracy would not permit.
A Commitment to User-Centered Design
This partnership translated into a fundamental change in how the municipality operated. The most significant lesson learned was not technical but philosophical, representing a reversal of the traditional bureaucratic mindset. A city official articulated this core operational shift:
"The most important lesson wasn’t technical. It was: never assume you know what people want until you sit with them."
This quote encapsulates the central tenet of Nakhon Si Thammarat's implementation strategy. It reflects the deep institutionalization of co-design, where prototyping, user testing, and direct citizen feedback became standard practice. This commitment ensured that every digital service was built with its end-users, not just for them.
Learning from Global Models
The city's internal culture also embraced a strategy of learning from international best practices without being confined by them. Leadership actively encouraged exposure to global innovation, not to clone solutions, but to broaden the team's understanding of what was achievable. As the mayor's advisor explained:
"We sent our staff to Korea, Japan, and even Eastern Europe to study municipal innovation. It wasn’t about copying. It was about seeing what’s possible."
This statement demonstrates a sophisticated approach to knowledge transfer. The city adapted concepts observed abroad—particularly from a study trip to South Korea—to fit its unique local context, leading to tailored innovations like the "AI School" and "Hospital on Wheels." This practice of intelligent adaptation allowed NST to leapfrog technologically while remaining grounded in its own reality.
These practical processes and partnerships were all guided by an overarching philosophy, articulated most clearly by the mayor himself.
4.0 The Guiding Philosophy: The Mayor's 'Clever Cats' Analogy

Source: Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa) and Ministry of Digital Economy and Society (2025). Citizen-First City Blueprint: An ASEAN Handbook for Leaders, Innovators, and Citizens. Slides generated via NotebookLM.
Beyond specific strategies, a guiding mindset is essential for leading profound and lasting change. In Nakhon Si Thammarat, this core philosophy—the "why" behind the technology—is the second layer of its transformation model. It is most clearly articulated through an analogy used by Mayor Kanop to explain his approach to leadership and change management.
Mayor Kanop's philosophy on earning buy-in and inspiring action is captured in what he calls the "Clever Cats' Analogy":
"You drag a cat forward, it will go backward. It resists change if you push or force. But if you are a stupid cat, if a mayor is a stupid cat, all the clever cats want to follow."
This analogy serves as a powerful metaphor for a leadership style rooted in humility and empowerment. It interprets "stupid" not as a lack of intelligence, but as a willingness to be vulnerable, to ask questions rather than issue commands, and to lead by showing a compelling path forward. The philosophy is to create a vision so clear and beneficial that talented and motivated people—the "clever cats"—naturally want to participate. This mindset of earning followership, rather than forcing it, was the invisible engine driving the city’s entire transformation.
This guiding principle underpins all the testimonies and themes presented in this report, leading to a clear set of conclusions about the nature of people-centric transformation.
5.0 Conclusion: A Narrative of People-Centric Transformation

Source: Digital Economy Promotion Agency (depa) and Ministry of Digital Economy and Society (2025). Citizen-First City Blueprint: An ASEAN Handbook for Leaders, Innovators, and Citizens. Slides generated via NotebookLM.
The collective testimonies of Nakhon Si Thammarat's citizens, officials, and partners weave a cohesive narrative of urban transformation built upon an intentional, multi-layered strategy. The qualitative evidence presented in this report confirms that the city's success is built upon a set of deeply human principles, enabled by structural reforms and a replicable operational methodology.
The key themes that emerge consistently from these voices include:
Enabling Governance: The foundation of change was a "Strong Mayor" governance model that provided the executive authority and direct accountability necessary for decisive leadership.
Authentic Leadership: Building trust is the non-negotiable first step. Lasting change is impossible without a leader who listens, demonstrates genuine commitment, and earns the public's confidence over the long term.
Citizen Co-Creation: The most effective solutions are designed with people, not for them. Starting with citizen pain points ensures relevance, adoption, and a shared sense of ownership.
A Culture of Learning: True innovation comes from humility, strategic partnership, and the intelligent adaptation of global knowledge. The city's collaborative ecosystem de-risked experimentation and accelerated progress.
Visionary Philosophy: Lasting change is guided by a clear, people-centric principle of empowerment, inspiring voluntary participation over forced compliance.
This transformation was not accidental; it followed a deliberate, five-step playbook: Pain Point → Purpose → Pilot → Solution → Scale. This process translates citizen frustrations into tangible, scalable solutions. Ultimately, these collective voices provide a powerful, human-centered narrative that confirms Nakhon Si Thammarat's journey is not an anomaly but a replicable model. It is a blueprint for how to build a smart city that places the dignity, needs, and wisdom of its citizens at the very heart of its purpose.
