11/20/25, 3:10 PM - Non: Here are some of the key ideas discussed in the meeting so far:

1. Human-Centric Smart City Development: Cities are focusing on solutions that are designed around the real needs and pain points of citizens, ensuring technology serves people—not the other way around.

2. Inclusive Leadership and Engagement: The success of technology adoption relies heavily on proactive, visible leadership and emphasis on inclusivity, community participation, and acknowledgment of diverse backgrounds and needs.

3. Digital Platforms for Urban Management: Implementation of city-wide digital tools (e.g., for handling resident complaints, water/flood management, public safety, and other issues) can drastically improve efficiency and citizen trust—examples include reducing service delivery times from weeks to hours.

4. Empowerment through Education and Health Technologies: Smart city efforts also target education (e.g., Metaverse classrooms, AR/VR, parental engagement with AI tutoring) and health (mobile clinics, integrated platforms) to improve quality of life for all citizens, especially seniors and underserved groups.

5. Public-Private Partnerships & Startups: Collaboration between governments and tech startups/small firms enables tailored solutions, cost savings, and rapid deployment compared to traditional large contractors.

6. Behavioral Change as a Core Challenge: Real change happens through leading by example ("show, don't tell"), making new systems easy and beneficial, leveraging word-of-mouth, social reinforcement, and keeping multiple options available for reluctant users.

7. Measuring and Celebrating Success: Recognizing achievements both locally and internationally (awards, public feedback) motivates further adoption and pride in these initiatives.

8. Leadership Strategies: Mayors and officials modeled "servant leadership," often doing essential work themselves to demonstrate commitment and inspire others, also using “fun,” “gamification,” and positive peer pressure to increase participation.

9. Scalability and Sustainability: The meeting raised questions and advice on how to scale these people-centric models to larger cities, considering the need for adaptable communication, local leadership, and sustainable processes.

11/21/25, 3:09 AM - Non: Summary of Today’s Smart City Workshop:

Today’s workshop focused on understanding how Nakhon Si Thammarat (NST) built a smart-city model that is people-centered, fast, and possible for any ASEAN city to adapt.

These were the main takeaways:


---

1. NST succeeds because it acts fast and starts small.

The city doesn’t wait for huge budgets or long procurement cycles.
It looks for problems that can be solved tomorrow, not in five years.
This agility is why startups and solution providers choose NST as a testbed.


---

2. Engagement and trust are the real “infrastructure.”

NST builds projects with citizens — not just for them.
High engagement levels come from:

transparency

approachable leadership

constant communication with communities
This is why new projects gain momentum quickly.



---

3. Replication requires understanding the principles, not copying the surface.

Participants noted that NST’s model cannot be cloned.
But the thinking behind it can be:

identify real pain points

start with low-cost, high-impact pilots

collaborate across departments

measure results and adjust
This mindset is more important than the specific technologies.



---

4. Simplicity beats complexity.

NST prioritizes systems that:

reduce workload

remove bureaucratic steps

solve clear, visible problems
Participants saw how simple tools — even basic sensors or apps — can unlock major improvements in safety, mobility, and services.



---

5. A smart city grows from evidence, not slogans.

NST uses data from:

floods

traffic

garbage

citizen behavior
to guide decisions.
Evidence replaces guesswork.
This is how the city avoids wasting money and time.



---

6. Every ASEAN city can apply these lessons in its own context.

No city starts from zero.
Today’s discussion showed three universal principles:

1. Start with what you have.


2. Fix one real problem first.


3. Use that success to build the next one.



NST grew its model by stacking small wins until they became a system.

11/21/25, 4:41 PM - Clinton: Governance & Systems
Some thoughts on framing:
•	Governance is more than the way that a government works systematically with its resources, finances, to govern, to deliver services.

•	We need to look at the philosophy of a city – it is actually a mindset, a culture, an ecosystem. Good governance is when public servants and the Mayor come to work, they know what needs to be done. The community is comfortable.

•	Overall, the goal is not just to deliver services and “govern” but to prepare and change the trajectory of a city.
Overall mission: Address structural requirements
•	We thought about Nakhon Si Thammarat context – but other cities aren’t at this level yet.

•	Across ASEAN, cities have spent years branding themselves as “smart,” but the agenda often masks deeper structural issues and is taken advantage of. Smart cities is often treated as a tech project but the balance goes too far towards tech solutions. Treat it as reorganising how the city functions and thinks.


1.	Map organizational structures
Overall point: A smart city goal is not enough, you need organisational resources and budgets - but if you don’t have enough, what do you do? How can you do “smart city” with few resources?
•	Some cities have Smart City Officers, a Mayor’s vision and plans, 3-4 smart city champions in each division.
•	Some cities have Smart City Labs (dedicated smart city offices set up in administration – 3-4 people).
•	“Smart city” policy sometimes sits in planning office, communications office, finance office, IT office – there are different approaches – but does it matter?
•	The way you bring in community engagement and consultations into the administration.

2.	Identify policy enablers and barriers
Enablers
•	Yes, structure is an enabler but…
•	Long-term discipline (a word not used in government much), not short-term showcases – if you’re going to organise a smart city event for 15 years – where are the results?
•	Think to 2100  - Future-oriented, long-term thinking that addresses long-term issues, such as: flooding, climate change, technology change (can’t anticipate everything, but can prepare and be open to change), economic changes (job creation, new industries?)
Barriers
•	Tough conversations, tough decisions
•	Winners and losers in anything
•	Overcome selfishness and self-interest
•	Staff that is not willing to change how they work – staff don’t want to use new systems, staff not wanting to take responsibility
•	Change the way that community talk with government – from ‘complaint’ mind to ‘city first’ mind – we are on the journey together, aren’t we?

3.	Document failure modes
•	Failures are a part of governance but many leaders are too scared to fail
•	Some cities take advantage of “smart city” branding but in practice are not
•	Recurring failures to do community engagement properly
Create governance recommendations
•	A story on shadowing or contacting a Mayor, particularly from a city such as Nakhon Si Thammarat – maybe it starts from a phone call…

The Real Problem Isn’t the Flood. It’s the System.

Thailand’s flooding mess isn’t mainly about extreme weather. It’s about institutional decay driven by politics. Sasin lays it out bluntly: the water system has collapsed because politics has eaten the professional chain of command.

1. Leadership is performing, not commanding.

When a crisis hits, the Prime Minister and ministers rush to flood zones, take photos, hand out goods, and look “present.”
But that’s exactly what a modern state should not do.

A modern state:

Runs a national War Room 24/7.

Operates with the same real-time data across agencies.

Makes decisions from radar, forecasts, hydraulic models — not from standing knee-deep in water for cameras.


Instead, we get scattered visits, zero unified data, and slow decisions.

2. Political appointments cripple technical agencies.

Key water and disaster agencies — Meteorology, Royal Irrigation, DDPM, Interior, ONWR — have leaders appointed for political loyalties, not expertise.

That creates:

Wrong forecasts from the start.

Slow orders because leaders don’t understand technical risk.

Experts being ignored or sidelined.

Agencies fighting each other instead of coordinating.


3. Experts are removed or excluded.

Those who actually understand water systems:

Are pushed out.

Are not invited to decision tables.

Get transferred during crises. This creates a “vacuum of expertise” right when decisions are most critical.


4. A culture of fear and face-saving.

Officials don’t report bad news because they “don’t want to upset the boss.”
They hesitate to order evacuation because they fear blame.
They avoid speaking up about systemic failures because it’s “inappropriate.”

In disaster management, hesitation kills capacity.
And this hesitation is structural, not personal.

5. Agencies do not share data.

Rivalries, personal conflicts, and a fractured bureaucracy mean:

Radar, storm tracks, field reports, reservoir data are not shared real-time.

Warnings reach governors or municipalities hours or days late.

Local governments act blind.


Floods are time-sensitive. A delay of 3 hours can turn a controllable event into a disaster.

6. Everything becomes political theatre.

The state prioritizes being seen over being effective.

Walking in water for photos.

Distributing relief bags.

Making symbolic visits.


It’s optics replacing governance.


---

Why Nakhon Si Thammarat’s Model Is the Better Way

NST City proves what happens when leadership and system design are right.

1. War Rooms that actually function as War Rooms.
There’s a real command system:

Staff see the same data.

Coordination is instant.

Information doesn’t get stuck in silos.

Leaders stay in the room instead of chasing cameras.


2. Staff communicate fluidly.
Systems allow teams to:

Share updates without friction.

Flag problems early.

Mobilize resources fast.


This kills bureaucratic lag.

3. Decisions are data-driven, not politically driven.
Leadership empowers experts.
This means:

Flood response starts before citizens even call for help.

Field teams deploy based on forecasts, not orders from above.

The city stays ahead of the disaster, not behind it.


4. Responsibility is structural, not personal.
NST City’s system reduces the need for heroism or political showmanship.
It institutionalizes competence.

5. It avoids the classic Thai failure: fragmentation.
NST’s model creates:

Shared situational awareness.

One picture of reality.

One rhythm of decision-making.


That’s what the national system lacks.


---

Bottom Line

Flooding in Thailand is not just a natural disaster.
It’s a political and institutional failure baked over decades.

Nakhon Si Thammarat City is showing the opposite approach:

Professional command,

Real-time data,

Empowered staff,

Rapid response,

Zero photo ops.


It’s what Thailand could look like if politics stepped back and systems stepped up..
